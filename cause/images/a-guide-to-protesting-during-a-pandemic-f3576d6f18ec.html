A Guide to Protesting During a Pandemic

How to minimize risk while showing support on the streets

As the coronavirus pandemic raged on this weekend, massive crowds gathered across the United States to protest the murder of George Floyd and countless other Black people at the hands of the police. As protesters marched, health officials raised concerns that the protests would spark another wave of coronavirus cases.

There’s no question that going out into the streets to protest in large groups of people raises your risk of catching and spreading the coronavirus. For many people, being a part of a protest against racism is a risk worth taking. But that’s not to say there aren’t ways to protest while minimizing your risk and protecting the safety of your fellow protesters.

If you’re going to protest, know the facts. Though many parts of the country have reopened, the coronavirus is still circulating, resulting in new positive cases every day.

What we know about it hasn’t changed: The virus is still primarily spread via droplets that we exhale when we talk, cough, sneeze, and shout. It’s still safest to stay six feet apart to minimize your chances of inhaling those droplets or breathing them onto other people. There’s still no established treatment or vaccine for Covid-19, and the disease can be severe — in some cases even fatal. Though older people seem to be more prone to severe disease, many young people end up hospitalized.

Health experts interviewed by NPR on Monday reiterated that any situation where people are in prolonged close contact raises the risk of spreading the coronavirus. Protests, where people march shoulder to shoulder and continually raise their voices, are a prime example of a situation where the disease can spread. While some experts have pointed out that being outdoors may mitigate some of the risk, as breezes can blow virus particles away, others think that protests are so densely packed with people that a breeze wouldn’t make much difference.

Wear a mask

One thing we do know is that masks work. They’re not perfect, but they offer important protection from breathing in viral particles and spreading them. Remember, it’s possible to be infected with the coronavirus and be asymptomatic, and if you are, you’re still capable of spreading the virus to fellow protesters.

A viral thread on Twitter shared by a nurse, Saba M., suggested also bringing an extra mask: “Yours will get hot and sweaty so switching it out halfway through is smart. If you have a cloth mask throw a bandana on top too!” If you must pull down your mask to take a drink of water or eat, she suggests doing so away from the crowd, because you are very exposed when you do so.

Make noise without yelling

In a Twitter thread, epidemiologist Eleanor Murray, an assistant professor of epidemiology at Boston University School of Public Health, pointed out that yelling can spread virus-containing droplets. Previous research has suggested that even loud talking disperses more viral particles than normal speech. If you protest, Murray suggests you “choose signs, drums, or similar noise makers.”

Stick with a buddy group

The rules of social distancing say we should stay as isolated as possible to avoid spreading the virus to other people or catching it from strangers. That’s obviously not possible at a protest, but there are ways to minimize your risk. Sticking with the same group of people—and knowing how much they’ve previously been exposed to the virus—can help you stay aware of your own risk of exposure. The questions you would ask people you want to “quaranteam” with are worth asking the members of your buddy group. (Also, it’s generally always a good idea to stick together during a protest.)

Wear eye protection

Murray also suggested wearing eye protection at a protest. Though close contact between people remains the primary way that the coronavirus spreads, CDC guidance states that “it may be possible that a person can get COVID-19 by touching a surface or object that has the virus on it and then touching their own mouth, nose, or possibly their eyes.” Wearing eye protection like sunglasses or goggles may also help shield your eyes from physical objects, like rubber bullets or debris that’s being thrown around.

Consider self-quarantining afterward

In a Twitter reply, Murray noted that you should consider self-quarantining after protesting. If you are infected with the coronavirus during the protest, it could take three to 14 days for symptoms to appear, or you may be asymptomatic. Either way, you may be able to spread the virus to people you come in contact with.

Avoid protesting in large groups if you live with older adults and immunocompromised people

Before going out to a large protest, consider the people you will be coming home to. Exposing yourself to crowds puts them at risk. This is especially important to consider if you live with older adults or immunocompromised people, who are at higher risk of severe illness if they are infected with the coronavirus.

Bring hand sanitizer and gloves

You can become infected if your hands pick up viral particles (from signs, water bottles, other people, etc.) and you touch your nose, mouth, or eyes. Avoid touching your face, and use hand sanitizer to regularly disinfect your hands. Wash them for 20 seconds with soap and water as soon as it becomes possible.

Gloves can serve as a barrier between your hands and viral particles, though the same rules still apply: Don’t touch your face, even with a gloved hand, and wash your hands with soap and water afterward. Heat-resistant gloves are useful to protect your hands from getting burned by open flames.

A positive antibody test doesn’t mean you’re immune

You may have recently taken an antibody test, which tells you whether or not you have antibodies against the coronavirus in your bloodstream. Even if you tested positive for antibodies, this does not mean you are totally immune to the virus, so you may still be able to get infected and spread the disease. Experts still don’t know whether having antibodies confers strong or long-lasting immunity.

Drink water

Staying hydrated is important for your overall health. Try not to share your water bottle to minimize the risk of spreading the virus.

Don’t protest in large groups if you’re sick

The urge to show support can be great — so great that you may want to protest even if you feel sick. But if you have a fever, cough, shortness of breath, muscle pain, or any of the other symptoms of Covid-19, the best way to show support is prevent other protesters from getting sick by staying home.
